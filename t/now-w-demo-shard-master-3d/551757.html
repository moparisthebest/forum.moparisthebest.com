I recommend not using fixed point for the depth buffer. Fixed point arithmetic distributes your numerical precision linearly across all available bits, whereas floating point values have a sliding precision (hence the name). And, in fact, floats have their best precision between values of 0 and 1. If you normalize your depth values between 0 and 1 or -1 and 1 (both of which are pretty common) using floats, you‚Äôll get much higher numeric accuracy than if you use fixed point. You can still run into z-fighting issues when dealing with very large draw distances, but there are other solutions to solve that (such as logarithmic z-buffering).

If you want to solve this efficiently, triangle clipping is the way to do it. It‚Äôs complicated and annoying to get right, but it‚Äôs likely cheaper than the alternative, which is per-pixel clipping. Methods:

  1. Per-pixel clipping: rasterize the entire triangle regardless of the coordinates (ie, do not force the coordinates to be within the screen coordinates). On a per-pixel basis, only write the pixel to the color buffer if the x/y values are within the screen. This is pixel-perfect clipping.

Pros: it‚Äôs very easy and quick to implement.
Cons: it leads to a lot of extra pixels being rendered that never show up (similar concept to overdraw; it‚Äôs just bad). Interpolation is expensive and we don‚Äôt want to do it if we are just going to throw away the result. Also, this method easily leads to the problem of triangles really close to the projection plane just bogging down your render time. If you get too close to some, they‚Äôll go so large that your rasterizer will practically be in an infinite loop.

  1. Per-triangle clipping: this involves using one of many different algorithms that clip the triangle (probably in view space) into 1 or 2 new triangles. It computes the vertex attribute values for the new vertices so that the interpolated pixels that end up being on the screen end up having the correct values. In particular, you would be clipping against your viewing frustum, rather than your screen. Since the screen is just one plane in the viewing frustum, this gives you nearly pixel perfect screen clipping, as well (which is the ideal result). You would still do screen clipping on top of this (you pretty much must have screen clipping), but you would be throwing away like 1 pixel per scanline on the border, rather than possibly thousands.

In terms of writing a practical software rasterizer, you basically need to do both. Here is a list of triangle clipping algorithms:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clipping_(computer_graphics)#Algorithms
